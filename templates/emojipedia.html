Breakfast: SPAM, Eggs, and Rice (with Furikake) The classic breakfast of Hawai’i! My favorite way to have it is to add a bunch of ketchup to the rice too.

Lunch: Saimin/Ramen In Hawai'i we have our own version of ramen that we call “saimin” which originates from the 1800s, the sugarcane era. A soft boiled egg is more common in ramen in Japan, but it’s a tasty addition I couldn’t leave out.

Dinner: Laulau, Poi, Lomi Lomi Salmon, and 'Āweoweo Laulau is usually made with a piece of pork and salted butterfish that get wrapped in lu’au (taro) leaves and then wrapped in ti leaves and steamed. The ti leaf isn’t eaten (it’s used as a package to hold in the steam). Poi, a staple starch in the Hawaiian diet, is made from the taro root that is mashed and pounded with water before being fermented. Lomi lomi salmon is named for the Hawaiian word “lomi-lomi” which means “to massage” as the ingredients are gently massaged or mixed together by hand. 'Āweoweo (“ah vay oh vay oh”) also known as the Hawaiian glasseye fish, has a special place in my heart. When I was growing up my grandma would always tell me stories about ‘āweoweo and how her favorite time to eat it was when my grandpa and his friends would go spearfishing to catch them. Undoubtedly it’s a delicious fish, but I recently saw old photos of my grandpa when he was in his early twenties with his prized catch. So this is my Hawaiian dinner plate full of ‘ono grindz (delicious food).

Streetfood: Hanami and Mitarashi Dango Mochi will always have my heart. This Japanese chewy sweet dessert made from pounded rice was always my favorite while I was growing up. When I finally got to travel to Japan for the first time, the only thing I wanted to eat was mochi of all kinds and I have fond memories of eating fresh kinako dango (mochi on a stick covered in sweet soybean flour) on Nakamise-dori in Asakusa and yakidango (mochi grilled over a charcoal fire with soy sauce flavor) and an-dango (mochi covered in sweet azuki beans) at Habutae Dango in Nippori.

Dessert: Shave Ice Shave ice is Hawaii’s version of Japanese kagigori, a dessert made of ice shavings that are topped with homemade syrups. My favorite shave ice has to be from Shimazu’s in Oahu because they have the best azuki beans that they put on the bottom with a scoop of vanilla ice cream and amazing haupia (coconut pudding) cream, mochi, and li hing mui (dried sour plum powder) that you can get on top.

Cafe: Coconut Coffee While Kona coffee is definitely famous, coconut coffee isn’t exactly traditional, but it was my fun way to make a distinctly Hawaiian style cafe emoji. Although I have been known to make coffee with coconut milk and a little bit of coconut syrup.

Nightlife: Disco/Mirror Ball Not Hawaiian or Japanese themed, but I had to make my own version of the disco ball (or mirror ball) emoji. Who doesn’t love a good ol’ time down at the discotheque though?

Bar or Pub: Tiki Drink The history of “tiki culture” is honestly a bit controversial and there are quite a lot of Native Hawaiians who don’t like it. But as someone who is part Hawaiian and grew up on the mainland, I always enjoyed seeing the different ways people celebrated Polynesian culture through their love of tiki drinks in modern times. Just be sure to stay educated, kids.

Shopping: International Marketplace Bag The International Marketplace in Waikiki is sadly no longer the magical place it was when I was a kid. My grandma and mom used to have a business selling bags and so I designed a shopping bag emoji inspired by the old International Marketplace, reminiscent of one of the beautiful bags they designed.

Grocery Store: The Thank You Bag Inspired by the pink ‘Thank You’ shopping bags of my childhood this grocery bag is full of Hawaiian sweet rolls, sugarcane, and a small bunch of apple bananas.

Convenience Store: Ube Slush I’ll confess, I really love to visit convenience stores in other countries. From Zabka in Poland, to corner shops in the UK, to the magical world of the konbini in Japan, I love convenience stores. They are great for just grabbing something quick to drink or snack on. So I needed to have this category and represent it with the mystical ube slush that, as far as I know, is only found at a certain popular convenience store chain in Hawai’i.

Farmer’s Market/Outdoor Market: Bolga Basket of Goods I love to find a local farmer’s market when I’m traveling because you can always find some wonderful local food and great fresh produce. I have an African bolga basket at home that I use for every trip to the grocery store and farmer’s market, so I had to include it in the default emojis. This bolga basket is full of Molokai hot bread, a golden Maui pineapple, and a bundle of red ginger and heliconia flowers. It’s an ode to my favorite finds at Hawaiian farmer’s markets and my Grandma who loves Hawaiian flowers and worked at a pineapple cannery as a teenager.

Bazaar/Covered Market: Crates of Papayas If it isn’t obvious, I love to find great food when I travel. When it comes to food I’ve found that covered markets and bazaars are in a class of their own. From the mercados of Mexico to the spice bazaars of Türkiye, you can find so many amazing things in the labyrinths that are covered markets. The crates of papayas represent the food sections in covered markets but so many markets are full of things beyond food and they all hold a special place in my heart.

Bookstores/Libraries: Book I will admit that I like to do a bit of shopping when I travel, but I especially like to check out local bookstores and libraries. Sometimes I can’t speak the language, but I love to see what treasures local bookstores hold. There are times you’ll find secret cafes inside, or beautiful postcards made by a local artist, or sometimes you’ll find the most perfect nook with a fireplace and comfy armchair in a bookstore that seems chaotically full of books, or you may stumble upon a local poetry reading, or the bookstore or library itself will be in the most beautifully designed building. I don’t always bring a book home with me, just because of attempting to live out of a backpack while traveling, but bookstores and libraries will always be one of my favorite types of places to visit.

Train: Shinkansen This train emoji is my own version of the Google bullet train emoji, but it’s style is reminiscent of the 0 Series Kodama shinkansen. My partner is a big train otaku (fan of trains) and while I have yet to actually take the shinkansen, I do love the Japanese trains I’ve been on. Trains of all kinds are always fun and I feel like it’s important to be able to mark the train stations you have to transit through or transfer at when you travel.
